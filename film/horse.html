The Horse in Motion

One of the earliest motion pictures is "The Horse in Motion," a series of photographs taken by English photographer Eadweard Muybridge in 1878. Muybridge was hired by Leland Stanford, a wealthy racehorse owner and former governor of California, to settle a bet about whether a galloping horse ever had all four feet off the ground at once. To answer the question, Muybridge created a complex system of cameras and trip wires, which he set up along the side of a racetrack. As the horse galloped past, each camera was triggered in sequence, capturing a series of still photographs that showed the horse's motion at regular intervals. He then used a device called a zoopraxiscope to project the images in sequence, creating the illusion of motion. The resulting series of photographs proved beyond a doubt that all four of the horse's hooves did indeed leave the ground at the same time during a gallop.

While "The Horse in Motion" was not a true movie, it showed that motion could be broken down into a series of discrete moments, each captured in a single photograph. This idea was fundamental to the development of motion pictures, which rely on the same basic principle of capturing a series of still images in sequence to create the illusion of motion.
